The eras of premodern Japan

Eight eras, one continuous story — from the arrival of Buddhism to the fall of the shogunate. Each is a short overview of what defined the age and why it mattered; explore the battles, people and events themselves on the map.

Asuka–Nara Heian Genpei Kamakura Nanboku-chō / Muromachi Sengoku Edo Bakumatsu

Asuka–Nara: Buddhism, the Imperial State, and the First Permanent Capitals

552–794

The Asuka–Nara period saw Japan transformed from a confederation of clans into a centralized state modeled on Tang China. Its conventional starting point is the official transmission of Buddhism from the Korean kingdom of Baekje, dated by the Nihon Shoki to 552. The new faith provoked conflict between the pro-Buddhist Soga and the rival Mononobe and Nakatomi, resolved by the Soga victory of 587. Under Empress Suiko, the regent Prince Shōtoku promoted Buddhism and Chinese-style governance, traditionally credited with the Twelve Cap Ranks (603) and the Seventeen-Article Constitution (604). The Taika Reform, beginning with the coup of 645 against Soga no Iruka, asserted imperial authority over land and people. The Jinshin War of 672 brought Emperor Temmu to power, and the Taihō Code of 701 established a comprehensive legal framework. In 710 the court founded its first enduring capital at Heijō-kyō (Nara). The era produced the Kojiki (712) and Nihon Shoki (720) and the great bronze Buddha of Tōdai-ji, dedicated in 752, while Buddhist temples grew politically influential. The capital moved to Nagaoka-kyō in 784 and finally to Heian-kyō (Kyoto) in 794.

The Heian Period: Court Culture and the Rise of the Warriors

794–1185

The Heian period opened in 794, when Emperor Kanmu moved the capital to Heian-kyō (present-day Kyoto), and it gives its name to nearly four centuries of aristocratic rule. Real power gradually passed from the throne to the Fujiwara, who wed their daughters to emperors and governed as regents (sesshō and kampaku); the line effectively began in 858, when Fujiwara no Yoshifusa took the regency for the child emperor Seiwa, and reached its height under Fujiwara no Michinaga around 1016. After official embassies to Tang China were discontinued in 894, the court turned inward and a distinctive native culture flowered, expressed through the kana syllabary in works such as Murasaki Shikibu's Tale of Genji (c. 1008) and Sei Shōnagon's Pillow Book, infused with the aesthetic of mono no aware. From 1086 retired emperors asserted authority through cloistered rule (insei), eroding Fujiwara dominance. Meanwhile provincial warrior houses grew strong: the Hōgen (1156) and Heiji (1159) disturbances brought Taira no Kiyomori to power, and the ensuing Genpei War (1180–1185) ended with Minamoto no Yoritomo's victory at Dan-no-ura in 1185, closing the Heian age and inaugurating warrior government.

The Genpei War and the Birth of Warrior Rule

1180–1185

The Genpei War (1180–1185) was the civil war that ended the dominance of the Taira (Heike) clan and brought the Minamoto (Genji) to power, inaugurating Japan's age of warrior rule. It began in 1180 when Prince Mochihito and Minamoto no Yorimasa called for resistance to Taira no Kiyomori, whose grandson, the child-emperor Antoku, sat on the throne. Their rising was crushed at the First Battle of Uji, but the revolt spread across the provinces, drawing in Minamoto no Yoritomo in the east and his cousin Kiso (Minamoto) Yoshinaka. Kiyomori died of illness in 1181. Yoshinaka shattered the Taira at Kurikara in 1183 and drove them from Kyoto, but his lawlessness in the capital led Yoritomo's brothers Noriyori and Yoshitsune to defeat and kill him at Awazu in 1184. Yoshitsune then routed the Taira at Ichi-no-Tani (1184) and Yashima (1185) before annihilating their fleet at Dan-no-ura in 1185, where Antoku drowned. Yoritomo, ruling from Kamakura, secured the right to appoint provincial constables and stewards in 1185, laying the foundation of the shogunate he would formally head as shōgun in 1192.

The Kamakura Period: Japan's First Warrior Government

1185–1333

The Kamakura period opened when Minamoto no Yoritomo, victor of the Genpei War (1180–1185), established a warrior government, the bakufu, at Kamakura in eastern Japan. By 1185 he had secured the right to appoint military governors (shugo) and estate stewards (jitō) nationwide, and in 1192 the court named him sei-i taishōgun. Power did not remain with the Minamoto line: after Yoritomo's death in 1199, his Hōjō in-laws came to dominate as hereditary regents (shikken), ruling in the name of figurehead shoguns. The Hōjō crushed retired emperor Go-Toba's rising in the Jōkyū War (1221), tightening warrior control over the court, and in 1232 issued the Goseibai Shikimoku (Jōei Code), the first legal code written for the samurai class. The regime repelled two Mongol invasions under Kublai Khan, in 1274 and 1281, when storms later mythologized as kamikaze helped scatter the fleets. Yet the ruinous defense costs, paid with few new lands to reward vassals, eroded loyalty. The era also saw vigorous new Buddhist movements—Pure Land, Nichiren, and Zen. In 1333, forces backing Emperor Go-Daigo, led by Ashikaga Takauji and Nitta Yoshisada, destroyed the Kamakura shogunate.

Nanboku-chō / Muromachi

1333–1467

In 1333 the warriors Ashikaga Takauji, Nitta Yoshisada, and Kusunoki Masashige helped Emperor Go-Daigo overthrow the Kamakura shogunate, launching the Kenmu Restoration (1333–1336) of direct imperial rule. The settlement collapsed quickly: Takauji turned against Go-Daigo, installed a rival emperor (Kōmyō), promulgated the Kemmu shikimoku in 1336, and in 1338 was named shogun, seating his government in Kyoto. Go-Daigo withdrew to Yoshino, creating a Southern Court that contested the Kyoto-based Northern Court. This Nanboku-chō ("Southern and Northern Courts") schism, with its shifting warrior allegiances, persisted until the courts were reconciled in 1392 under the third shogun, Ashikaga Yoshimitsu. The regime is called Muromachi after the Kyoto district where the Ashikaga seated their headquarters from the 1370s. Under Yoshimitsu (r. 1368–1408) shogunal authority peaked: he dominated the provincial shugo, opened tribute trade with Ming China, and patronized the Kitayama culture epitomized by the Golden Pavilion (Kinkaku-ji), Zen, and the noh theatre. Yet the bakufu never fully mastered the great provincial lords. Mounting succession and factional disputes erupted in 1467 into the Ōnin War, which devastated Kyoto and ushered in the Sengoku age of endemic civil war.

The Sengoku Period: Japan's Age of Warring States

1467–1600

The Sengoku ("Warring States") period opened with the Ōnin War (1467–1477), a succession dispute within the Ashikaga shogunate that devastated Kyoto and shattered central authority. Over the following century, the country fragmented into domains ruled by competing warlords—the sengoku daimyō—who built castles, codified house laws, and waged near-constant war. Social upheaval was so pervasive that retainers and peasants frequently overthrew their superiors, a phenomenon contemporaries called gekokujō ("the low overcoming the high").

The era was also one of contact and exchange. Portuguese traders reached Tanegashima in 1543, introducing the matchlock firearm, which reshaped warfare; the Jesuit Francis Xavier began Christian missions in 1549. From the 1560s, Oda Nobunaga rose to dominate central Japan, expelling the last Ashikaga shogun in 1573, before his death at the Honnō-ji Incident in 1582. His successor, Toyotomi Hideyoshi, completed the military unification of Japan by 1590, though he never held the title of shogun. After Hideyoshi's death in 1598, the period closed with Tokugawa Ieyasu's decisive victory at the Battle of Sekigahara in 1600, which paved the way for the Tokugawa shogunate established in 1603.

The Edo Period: Tokugawa Peace and Seclusion

1603–1853

The Edo period began in 1603, when Emperor Go-Yōzei appointed Tokugawa Ieyasu as shogun, establishing a bakufu centred on Edo (modern Tokyo). Ieyasu secured the dynasty by abdicating to his son Hidetada in 1605 and by destroying the rival Toyotomi house in the sieges of Osaka (1614–1615). The Tokugawa imposed a durable order on the daimyō through institutions largely consolidated under the third shogun, Iemitsu: the system of alternate attendance (sankin-kōtai), formalized in 1635, and the maritime restrictions (sakoku) completed by 1639, which curtailed foreign contact and largely confined trade to a Dutch and Chinese presence at Nagasaki. Christianity, banned from 1614, was suppressed after the Shimabara Rebellion of 1637–1638. More than two centuries of internal peace followed. Agriculture and commerce expanded, cities grew, and a vibrant urban townsman (chōnin) culture flourished, epitomized by the Genroku era (c. 1688–1704): the haiku of Matsuo Bashō, the fiction of Ihara Saikaku, the puppet and kabuki theatre of Chikamatsu Monzaemon, and the ukiyo-e print. The period's seclusion ended in 1853, when Commodore Matthew Perry's American squadron arrived at Uraga, forcing Japan toward foreign trade and the upheavals that would topple the shogunate.

Bakumatsu: The Fall of the Tokugawa Shogunate

1853–1869

The Bakumatsu, or "end of the shogunate," was the turbulent final phase of Tokugawa rule. It opened in July 1853 when Commodore Matthew Perry's American "Black Ships" anchored off Uraga and demanded that Japan abandon its policy of restricted foreign contact (sakoku). The shogunate yielded with the Convention of Kanagawa in 1854, opening Shimoda and Hakodate, and in 1858 signed the Harris Treaty and other "unequal treaties" granting foreigners extraterritoriality and fixed tariffs. These concessions exposed Tokugawa weakness and inflamed a movement under the slogan sonnō jōi ("revere the emperor, expel the barbarians"). Chief minister Ii Naosuke's repression of dissent ended with his assassination at the Sakurada Gate in 1860. Domains led by Satsuma and Chōshū, initially rivals, formed a secret alliance in 1866 and pressed for the shogun's overthrow. In late 1867 Tokugawa Yoshinobu resigned his authority (taisei hōkan); in January 1868 reformers proclaimed the "restoration of imperial rule" (ōsei fukkō) under the young Emperor Meiji. The ensuing Boshin War (1868–1869) crushed the remaining shogunal forces, ending at Hakodate in 1869. The period closed the feudal order and inaugurated the Meiji Restoration and Japan's rapid modernization.
