Sources & credits

This atlas is assembled from published scholarship, open historical-GIS datasets, and freely-licensed maps and images — each credited below. Coordinates and facts were cross-checked against Japanese and English Wikipedia.

Narrative & historical sources

The history — what happened, who, when, and why — is drawn primarily from:

  • The Cambridge History of Japan, Volumes 1–6 (Cambridge University Press), and The New Cambridge History of Japan, Volumes 2–3 — the principal scholarly narrative across all eras.
  • The Chronicle of Lord Nobunaga (Shinchō Kōki), trans. J. S. A. Elisonas & J. P. Lamers (Brill) — a primary source for the Oda era.
  • The Tale of the Heike — the classic account of the Genpei War.
  • The ancient chronicles — the Kojiki (712) and the Nihon Shoki (Nihongi, 720) — Japan's earliest histories, for the mythological and early-imperial periods.
  • The Hōjō Godaiki; Kaempfer's Japan (Tokugawa culture observed); Samurai Warfare; The Founding of the Kamakura Shogunate; and other specialist works.
  • Japanese and English Wikipedia — used throughout for verification, and as the primary source for battle/site coordinates.

Coordinates & geographic data

  • Japanese Wikipedia — the latitude/longitude given at the top of each battle, castle, and site article.
  • Historical GIS & place-name datasets — including the Harvard Japan Historical GIS and Ritsumeikan University's historical GIS, for cross-referencing locations.

Where a battlefield's exact location is not recorded, a best-estimate coordinate is used and flagged as approximate in the data.

Map data & licensing

Old-province boundaries (旧国)

The historical province outlines are from the Center for Open Data in the Humanities (CODH) 旧国・旧郡境界データセット (Historical Administrative Boundary Dataset), simplified for the web. Licensed CC BY-NC 4.0.

Important: the CODH boundary data is non-commercial (CC BY-NC). This atlas may be freely shared and used for education and study with attribution, but it — and any work reusing the province data — must remain non-commercial.

Historical (old) map overlays

The draped Genroku (1690), Sekisui (1779) and Ōedo (1860) maps are georeferenced scans from the Ritsumeikan University "Map Warper" project (mapwarper.h-gis.jp), which aligns historical maps to modern coordinates.

Images
