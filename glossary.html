Glossary

The key terms, titles and institutions of premodern Japan — the words behind the map. 34 entries.

tennō天皇

The emperor of Japan, regarded as the sovereign and ritual head of state descended from the sun goddess Amaterasu. For most of premodern history the emperor reigned but did not rule, real power lying with regents, retired emperors, or military leaders.

kuge公家

The civil court aristocracy of Kyoto who served the imperial court and held ceremonial offices and ranks. Distinct from the warrior class (buke), they retained prestige and cultural influence even after political power passed to the samurai.

sesshō摂政

A regent who governed on behalf of an emperor who was a child or otherwise unable to rule. The office was monopolized for centuries by the Fujiwara family.

kampaku関白

A chief adviser and regent acting on behalf of an adult emperor, the civil counterpart of the sesshō. Like the sesshō it was dominated by the Fujiwara; Toyotomi Hideyoshi later held the title as a non-Fujiwara.

shōgun将軍

Short for sei-i taishōgun ("barbarian-subduing great general"), the supreme military commander who, from 1192, was the de facto ruler of Japan under nominal imperial authority. The office was held in turn by the Minamoto, Ashikaga, and Tokugawa houses.

bakufu幕府

Literally "tent government," the military government headed by the shōgun, often rendered "shogunate." Japan had three: the Kamakura (1192–1333), Muromachi/Ashikaga (1336–1573), and Tokugawa/Edo (1603–1868).

shikken執権

The regent who actually controlled the Kamakura shogunate while the shōgun served as a figurehead. The post was held hereditarily by the Hōjō family.

shugo守護

A military governor appointed by the shogunate to oversee one or more provinces, responsible for policing, military levies, and enforcing shogunal authority. Over time many shugo built independent regional power bases.

jitō地頭

A land steward appointed by the shogunate to manage estates (shōen), collect taxes, and maintain order at the local level. Jitō were a key instrument by which the warrior government extended control over the land.

samurai / bushi侍／武士

The hereditary warrior class of premodern Japan. "Bushi" is the broad term for warriors, while "samurai" originally meant those who served a lord; both came to denote the military aristocracy that dominated society from the late Heian period to 1868.

daimyō大名

A great territorial lord who held extensive lands and commanded a body of samurai retainers. Under the Tokugawa, daimyō ruled domains (han) assessed at 10,000 koku or more and were subordinate to the shōgun.

ashigaru足軽

Light-armed foot soldiers, typically of peasant origin, who formed the infantry of warrior armies. Their numbers and use of pikes and matchlock guns grew decisively in the Sengoku period.

rōnin浪人

A masterless samurai who had lost or left the service of a lord, through the lord's death, defeat, or dismissal. Many became wanderers, mercenaries, or sources of social unrest, especially in the early Edo period.

sōhei僧兵

Armed warrior-monks attached to powerful Buddhist temples and monasteries such as Enryaku-ji and Kōfuku-ji. They formed temple militias that could exert military and political pressure, even on the capital.

ikki一揆

A league or uprising, ranging from sworn alliances of warriors or villagers to large armed revolts. Notable forms include peasant tax revolts (do-ikki) and the religiously organized Ikkō-ikki of the Jōdo Shinshū sect.

seppuku切腹

Ritual suicide by cutting the abdomen, performed by samurai to preserve honor, atone for failure, avoid capture, or as a sentence of death. Also colloquially called harakiri, it was governed by strict ceremony, often with an assistant who beheaded the participant.

kokudaka石高

The assessed productive value of a piece of land expressed in koku of rice, used to gauge the wealth, tax obligation, and rank of domains and retainers. The system underpinned landholding and stipends throughout the Edo period.

koku石

A unit of volume of about 180 liters, nominally the amount of rice needed to feed one person for a year. It served as the standard measure of agricultural yield, stipends, and a daimyō's status.

han藩

A domain governed by a daimyō under the Tokugawa shogunate, the basic unit of regional administration. Each han had a degree of internal autonomy while owing allegiance and obligations to the shōgun.

sankin-kōtai参勤交代

The Tokugawa system of "alternate attendance" requiring daimyō to spend alternating periods in residence at the shōgun's capital of Edo and in their domains. Their families were kept in Edo as effective hostages, and the costs of travel and dual residences curbed daimyō wealth and power.

yamajiro山城

A mountain castle built on a hilltop or ridge, relying on steep natural terrain for defense. This was the dominant fortress type during the medieval and early Sengoku eras.

hirajiro平城

A castle built on flat, low-lying ground, typically in or near a town and defended by moats, walls, and ramparts rather than terrain. Such castles became common in the late Sengoku and Edo periods as administrative centers.

mizujiro水城

A castle sited on or beside water—a lake, river, sea, or marsh—using the water as a moat and defensive barrier. The term is sometimes treated as a subtype of the flatland (hirajiro) castle.

hirayamajiro平山城

A "flatland-mountain" castle built on an isolated hill or low rise above a plain, combining the defensibility of a hilltop with proximity to towns and roads. Many of Japan's great Sengoku and Edo castles, such as Himeji, are of this type.

tenshu天守

The central keep or main tower of a Japanese castle, the tallest structure and a symbol of a lord's authority. It served as a watchtower and last redoubt, though daily administration usually took place in surrounding buildings.

Sengoku jidai戦国時代

The "Warring States period," roughly 1467–1568 (extending to 1600 in broader usage), an era of near-constant civil war following the collapse of Ashikaga authority. Regional daimyō fought for supremacy until unification was achieved by Oda Nobunaga, Toyotomi Hideyoshi, and Tokugawa Ieyasu.

gekokujō下剋上

Literally "the lower overthrowing the higher," the social upheaval of the Sengoku era in which retainers, lesser warriors, or commoners displaced their superiors. It typified the period's collapse of established hierarchies.

kamikaze神風

The "divine winds," the typhoons that wrecked the Mongol invasion fleets of 1274 and 1281 and saved Japan from conquest. The events fostered a belief that Japan was a divinely protected land; the term was later revived for World War II suicide pilots.

Bakumatsu幕末

The "end of the shogunate," the turbulent final years of Tokugawa rule (roughly 1853–1868) opened by the arrival of Commodore Perry's American fleet. It saw foreign pressure, domestic political crisis, and the movements that led to the Meiji Restoration.

sonnō jōi尊王攘夷

The political slogan "revere the emperor, expel the barbarians," rallying anti-Tokugawa and anti-foreign activists during the Bakumatsu. It championed restoring imperial authority and resisting Western intrusion, and it helped galvanize the movement to overthrow the shogunate.

taisei hōkan大政奉還

The "return of political power," the formal restoration of governing authority to the emperor by the last shōgun, Tokugawa Yoshinobu, in 1867. It ended the Tokugawa shogunate and paved the way for the Meiji Restoration.

ōsei fukko王政復古

The "restoration of imperial rule," proclaimed in January 1868, which abolished the shogunate and regency offices and declared direct rule by the emperor. It inaugurated the Meiji government that began modernizing Japan.

Meiji Ishin明治維新

The Meiji Restoration of 1868, the political revolution that ended the Tokugawa shogunate and restored the emperor as head of a centralized state. It launched Japan's rapid modernization and is conventionally taken to close the premodern era.

katanagari刀狩

The "sword hunt," Toyotomi Hideyoshi's 1588 policy of confiscating weapons from peasants, monks, and commoners. Together with the cadastral surveys it sharpened the legal divide between the warrior class and the farming population.
